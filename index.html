Obesity in America: Trends Across Age, Geography, and Income (2011–2023)

Obesity prevalence in the United States has increased steadily over the past decade, but the magnitude and distribution of change differ across demographic and socioeconomic groups.
A data story by Junzhe Shen & Jiawei Ji · Data source: CDC BRFSS (2011–2023)

Age Patterns Indicate a Shifting Onset of Obesity Risk

Historically, obesity was regarded as a condition that emerged predominantly in midlife. Contemporary evidence indicates that elevated risk now appears earlier across successive cohorts, lengthening the cumulative duration of exposure and underscoring the importance of age-specific trajectories.

Age-Related Baseline Differences

At the outset of the study period, obesity prevalence increased steadily with age. Adults aged 45–64 showed the highest initial levels, whereas individuals aged 18–24 and 25–34 exhibited substantially lower starting values.

Rate of Change Across the Life Course

All age groups experienced increases from 2011 to 2023. Although older adults continued to display the highest absolute prevalence, younger adults—particularly those aged 18–24— recorded the steepest relative increases, suggesting earlier onset of elevated risk.

Implications for Long-Term Risk Exposure

By 2023, prevalence had risen across the full age spectrum. These shifts imply that newer cohorts spend more years living with elevated obesity risk, potentially compounding long-term health burden unless upstream determinants are addressed.

Geographic Variation Reveals Uneven Regional Trajectories

National averages conceal substantial spatial heterogeneity. Some states exceed 40% prevalence, while others remain markedly lower. Mapping these differences highlights the influence of local policy environments, socioeconomic conditions, and built infrastructure.

National Trends Over Time

The national average exhibits a consistent upward trajectory throughout the observation period, increasing from the upper 20s into the mid-30s with no indication of stabilization.

High-Burden Regional Clusters

States in the Deep South and Appalachian region demonstrate the highest prevalence, frequently surpassing 40%. These patterns reflect a constellation of structural barriers, resource limitations, and longstanding socioeconomic inequities.

Areas of Relative Resilience

Conversely, several coastal states and the District of Columbia maintain substantially lower rates. These differences underscore the role of policy investments, neighborhood environments, and access to preventive health supports in shaping population-level outcomes.

Income Gradients Highlight Shifts in Socioeconomic Risk Patterns

While obesity prevalence remains highest among lower-income groups, recent patterns reveal that higher-income populations are experiencing the most rapid increases. This shift suggests that upstream drivers of obesity are becoming more pervasive across socioeconomic strata.

Persistent Baseline Inequalities

Lower-income adults consistently show the highest obesity prevalence, reflecting enduring disparities tied to food availability, neighborhood environments, and structural health constraints.

Differential Growth Over Time

When examining proportional changes since 2011, higher-income groups exhibit the largest increases. These patterns indicate that environmental and behavioral risk factors have become more widespread, reducing the degree of socioeconomic insulation previously observed.

Broadening of Population-Level Risk

Rising prevalence across all income categories demonstrates that obesity is now a generalized population-wide challenge. This broadening emphasizes the need for policy approaches that address upstream determinants at scale across diverse socioeconomic contexts.

Final Reflections

Across age groups, geographic regions, and income levels, obesity prevalence in the United States has risen persistently over the past decade. Although the rate and magnitude of change vary across subpopulations, the overall trend is unequivocally upward, indicating a growing public health challenge with implications for long-term morbidity and health system burden.

These patterns collectively point to a structural shift rather than isolated demographic effects. Mitigating future risk will require interventions that address the environments in which dietary behaviors, physical activity, and long-term health trajectories are shaped.
